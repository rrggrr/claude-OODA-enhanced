# Boyd's Real OODA Loop: Theory and Sources

Read this when you want the grounding behind the skill, need to teach OODA
correctly, or want to push back on a popularized misuse. It documents the loop
Boyd actually drew — not the four-box circle.

## Who Boyd was

John R. Boyd (1927–1997), USAF Colonel and fighter pilot. Nicknamed "Forty
Second Boyd" for a standing bet that he could beat any pilot in simulated air
combat within 40 seconds from a position of disadvantage. He co-developed
Energy–Maneuverability (E-M) theory, which became the world standard for fighter
design and shaped the F-15 and F-16. The E-M insight — that the *rate of change*
of energy state matters more than peak performance — is the seed of OODA: what
beats you isn't being slower in the abstract, it's failing to transition through
states faster than your opponent can comprehend. Boyd wrote almost nothing for
publication; his ideas live in briefings he gave away for free and in one short
essay.

## The diagram Boyd actually drew (1995–96)

Boyd sketched the OODA diagram once, near the end of his life, in
**"The Essence of Winning and Losing"** (1995; final revision Jan 1996). It is far
richer than the popular circle. Key features:

- **Four phase labels** — Observe, Orient, Decide, Act — but Decide is annotated
  **"(Hypothesis)"** and Act **"(Test)."** Boyd is explicitly framing the loop as a
  scientific learn-by-experiment process, not a command sequence.
- **Observe** takes three inputs: *Unfolding Circumstances*, *Outside Information*,
  and *Unfolding Interaction with the Environment* — plus feedback from your own
  actions.
- **Orient** is drawn as the large, central element containing five interacting
  ingredients: **Genetic Heritage, Cultural Traditions, Previous Experience, New
  Information, and Analyses & Synthesis.** Boyd called Orientation the
  *Schwerpunkt* (focus) of the whole loop: in his words it "shapes the way we
  observe, the way we decide, the way we act." This is the textual basis for
  "Orientation dominates."
- **Implicit Guidance & Control (IG&C):** two arrows — from Orient directly to
  Observe, and from Orient directly to Act — that the cartoon omits. The first
  means orientation *filters perception* (you see what your model expects; experts
  see what novices miss; bias is the pathology). The second means skilled actors
  *act straight from orientation, bypassing Decide entirely*. This is why experts
  appear to react without deliberating.
- **Feed-forward and feedback** arrows run throughout: the phases operate
  concurrently and continuously update each other. Boyd put "Loop" in quotation
  marks; it is not a tidy circle. Chet Richards, who built the diagram with Boyd,
  argues the truest description is "Observe–Orient–Hypothesize–Test," with the
  explicit Decide step often skipped.

## The strategic aim: mismatches, not speed

From **"Organic Design for Command and Control,"** Boyd's stated goal is to
"operate inside the adversary's OODA loops … to enmesh the adversary in a world of
uncertainty, doubt, mistrust, confusion, disorder, fear, panic, chaos … and/or
fold the adversary back inside himself" so he can't cope with events as they
unfold. The decisive condition is the opponent turning *inward* — spending energy
trying to sort himself out instead of engaging the real situation. This is why the
skill insists tempo is about degrading the opponent's orientation, not raw speed.

## "Destruction and Creation" (1976): the epistemology

Boyd's only formal essay argues *why* mental models must be continually destroyed
and rebuilt. He invokes three results: **Gödel's incompleteness** (a system can't
verify its own consistency from within), **Heisenberg's uncertainty** (observing
from inside a system perturbs it), and the **second law of thermodynamics** (closed
systems trend to disorder). Conclusion: any orientation that only refines itself
internally drifts out of match with reality and generates its own confusion. The
escape is the dialectic of **destructive deduction** (shatter the existing concept
into parts) and **creative induction** (synthesize a new whole, often by importing
structure across domains — his "snowmobile"). This grounds the destruction-and-
creation tool in the orientation toolkit.

## The organizational layer (most-ignored, very important)

Boyd spent most of his briefings on *organizations*, not individual cognition. The
ability to run fast loops at scale depends on a **common implicit orientation**
plus the maneuver-warfare enablers: **Einheit** (mutual trust), **Auftragstaktik**
(mission command — direct via intent, not detailed orders), **Schwerpunkt** (shared
focus of effort), and **Fingerspitzengefühl** (intuitive feel from shared
experience). Without shared orientation and trust, IG&C can't operate across people
and the organization's loop slows to the speed of its approval chain.

## Misconceptions, stated plainly

1. **"OODA = go faster."** Wrong; it's orientation quality + mismatch generation.
   Boyd explicitly endorsed slowing down to set up a better action.
2. **"It's sequential."** Wrong; concurrent with feedback. "Military forces do not
   wait to observe until they have acted."
3. **"Decide is the key step."** Wrong; Orient is, and Decide is frequently
   bypassed via implicit guidance.
4. **"It's a model of decision-making."** Closer to the truth (per Osinga): it's a
   model of *learning and adaptation* — how orientation shapes perception and
   action over time.

## Modern echoes worth knowing

- **Business:** Chet Richards' *Certain to Win* applies Boyd to firms via
  asymmetric fast transients and lean/Toyota-style implicit coordination. The Lean
  Startup's Build–Measure–Learn cites Boyd but runs in a different (planning)
  direction — treat them as cousins, not twins.
- **Cybersecurity:** OODA sits *above* frameworks like the Cyber Kill Chain, MITRE
  ATT&CK, and F3EAD — those describe what an adversary does; OODA describes how any
  defender processes it. Practitioners model the *attacker's* OODA loop and ask
  which phase is cheapest to disrupt (often Orient, via deception). Detection
  latency is the dominant metric because attackers win by corrupting defender
  orientation faster than defenders refresh it.
- **Decision science / agile:** OODA pairs with **Cynefin** (match loop style to
  situation type) and contrasts with **PDCA** (Deming) — PDCA for stable,
  predictable improvement; OODA for volatile, adversarial, time-compressed
  contexts. In complex domains, run multiple parallel safe-to-fail loops, not one.
- **AI/automation:** recent scholarship (e.g., James Johnson, *Defence Studies*,
  2022) warns that automating the loop tends to hollow out *Orient* — the phase
  that carries human judgment, ethics, and context. When an AI hands you not just
  data but *meaning*, you risk absorbing its framing without genuinely orienting.
  A practical guard: at the Orient step ask "what assumptions is this making, what
  context does it lack, and am I orienting or just endorsing?"
